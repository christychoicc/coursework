1. Initial Project Scope

Background

Evictions are closely linked with economic hardships and social instability, often contibuting to homelessness and psychological distress, disproportionately impacting the more vulnerable population of the city. In New York City, around two-thirds of households rent their homes, with a large proportion considered themselves as housing cost burdened, spending 30-50% of income on rents (nyc.gov, 2024). With a context of high living costs and spatial inequality, this can further exacerbates eviction rates within the city.

Policies have been introduced in mitigation to strengthen tenants protection and avoid evictions. Most relevant to this analysis is the Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act 2019 (HSTPA), covering New York on the state level, aimed to strengthen eviction protection through limiting tenancy terminations, mandating longer notices periods, and a series of tenant rights protection measures. The analysis on evictions in 2020 therefore will be reflecting the housing market under this stricter regulatory and legal frameworks.

Furthermore, the year 2020 was largely affected by the COVID-19 pandemic, which intensified these pressures. This period precipitated a severe economic crisis in the United States, highlighting higher rates of unemployment and income drops. In response, the state introduced the Eviction Moratoriums measures, aimed to protect tenants from evictions due to the unforeseen circumstances such as failing to pay their rent. All things considered, these factors have motivated the analysis aims to give a better understanding of the spatial distribution of eviction patterns across community district boundaries of New York City, identifying areas of eviction concentration, supporting policy makers for informed decision making and strengthen renter tenancy protection measures.

Research Question

The following analysis will aim to answer the following research question: Which areas exhibits spatial hotspots clustering activity of residential evictions within the community district boundaries in New York City in 2020?

Data Evaluation

  • Evictions

    • Nature: point data with location, date, types of evictions

    • Strength: provided precise geocoded location (Latitude/Longitude) allowing for spatial analysis, and differentiates between Residential and Commercial tenancy types

    • Weaknesses: missing values in Latitude and Longitude columns, data only covered executed evictions where informal evictions were not available, population and household counts were not available which limits the calculation for eviction rates.

  • Community Districts

    • Nature: polygon data with administrative boundaries of 59 districts and 12 joint interest areas (JIAs)

    • Strength: official community boundaries used for planning and policies allowing analysis to be interpretable for policy recommendation purposes, standard CRS and metadata provided

    • Weaknesses: community districts varies in size aggregating data points on large polygons may risk modifiable areal unit problem (MAUP), JIAs refers to non-residential areas which may distort the analysis if not removed

Data Wrangling Explanation

  • Load in CSV and Shapefile

  • Convert CSV into sf object, latitude and longitude columns present in CSV

  • Transform CRS, make sure common CRS for both

  • Filter dates for year 2020 only

  • Perform a spatial join for evictions count per community district

Plan

  • Data Process

    • Data loading, cleaning, CRS projection, plot points and polygons for data inspection

  • Descriptive overview of eviction patterns

    • Bar chart - identify evictions counts per borough

  • Exploratory Point Pattern Analysis

    • Kernel Density Estimation to visually identify a broad initial identification of high and low eviction intensity and potential hotspot

  • Spatial Autocorrelation

    • Join eviction points with district polygons for a choropleth map of eviction density across New York

    • Global Morans I to test whether similar values (high or low) tend to cluster in space, testing global (overall) spatial autocorrelation

    • Local Morans I to identifies where local clustering of eviction intensity is strongest in New York supported with a z-score density map to identify clusters and outliers using queen contiguity weights

    • Local Getis-Ord Gi* Hotspot Map would be the final map to answer the research question

To include all points that share common features (share common corners or boundary) inclusively, the queen continguity is used to define spatial neighbours. Since observations from previous mappings have shown that New York boundaries included waterfront and island community districts which are separated by water. They may have no contiguous neighbours within the study area, therefore the zero.policy = TRUE is used to permit spatial units with no neighbours under this circumstance to allow them to be retained in the analysis.

#create a neighbours list
districtsW_nb <- evictions_density %>%
  #poly2nb --> find which districts touch each other
  poly2nb(., queen=T)
## Warning in poly2nb(., queen = T): some observations have no neighbours;
## if this seems unexpected, try increasing the snap argument.
## Warning in poly2nb(., queen = T): neighbour object has 4 sub-graphs;
## if this sub-graph count seems unexpected, try increasing the snap argument.
#create spatial weight matrix from these weights
districtsW_nb_queens <- districtsW_nb %>%
  nb2listw(., style="W", 
           #some observations have no neighbours (above code output) --> NYC boundaries have a river in between
           #in this case we allow districts with no neighbours!!
           zero.policy = TRUE)
#run Moran's I
morantest <- moran.test(evictions_density$density_km2, districtsW_nb_queens)

Spatial Autocorrelation - Getis-Ord Gi*

Previously, Local Morans I identified districts of local clustering. To answer the research question of identifying which areas exhibit hotspots residential eviction clustering, the Gi* statistic will be used to identify districts that clusters with high values relative to the global mean. The Gi* Z-scores were mapped using the same diverging palette and significance threshold as the Local Moran Z-scores. Community districts with large positive Gi* Z-scores (e.g.Z > 1.96) will be interpreted as statistically significant hotspots of eviction density, whereas large negative Z-scores (e.g.Z < 1.96) will be interpreted as cold spots.

The Gi* hotspot map identifies community districts with high value clustering (significantly high eviction density surrounded by similarly high-density neighbours) as hotspots, and districts with low value clustering (significantly low density surrounded by low-density neighbours) as cold spots.

There are several statistically significant hotspots are observed in Bronx, indicating spatially concentrated areas of eviction activity in 2020. In contrast, other than identified locations of hotspots, the cold spots are observed in most areas in New York, suggesting most parts of the city experience consistently low eviction density. These hotspot and cold-spot patterns answers the research question spatially, highlighting areas that should be targeted for eviction-prevention policy interventions priorities.

4. Conclusion

To conclude, the analysis have revealed that residential evictions in 2020 are not evenly spatial distributed but strongly spatially clustered across New York. Bronx as a hotspot was recorded for highest count and highest concentration, while other boroughs such as Staten Island and parts of Queens and Manhattan experienced relatively lower eviction activities as coldspots in the analysis.

Findings on community districts and mapping eviction density per km levels provided a more better understanding of patterns in spatial intensity. Eviction density was not randomly distributed but exhibited significant positive spatial autocorrelation, where districts with similar levels of eviction density tends to be located near each other. Tests on local levels identified specific areas with high eviction density with statistically significant local clusters as hotspots, and low eviction density areas as cold spots. All things considered, despite the year 2020 was shaped by stronger tenant protections under HSTPA and temporary COVID-19 eviction moratoria measures, there are still formal evictions were spatially concentrated in distinct neighbourhoods .

In a policy perspective, these findings suggests that policy makers should prioritise efforts in focusing in districts with high eviction density, for effective eviction prevention and renter tenancy protection measures across geographically targetted districts.

5. Reflection and Limitations

  • Limitation 1 (Data) : The analysis was limited to data for 2020 only, which may not be a full representation of eviction patterns across New York City.

  • Limitation 2 (Method) : Other potential factors such as population that will influence eviction rates and patterns were not considered in this analysis due to data limitations, which future analysis could indicate.

  • Limitation 3 (Temporal) : The year of 2020 was the period affected by the COVID-19 pandemic and eviction moratorium, therefore results may not be representative of eviction patterns across New York.
